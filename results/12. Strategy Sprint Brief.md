# OpenHuman — Strategy Sprint Brief

**Prepared for:** Founder-CEO, OpenHuman
**Prepared by:** McKinsey & Company — AI Infrastructure Practice
**Date:** May 2026
**Classification:** Board-ready, internal

---

## 1. Executive Summary

**Situation.** OpenHuman is pre-revenue, pre-distribution, and pre-model. The vision — a Pixar-style streaming digital human at <$0.001/user/month, with a custom protocol, custom render engine on three platforms, and a proprietary motion dataset extracted from YouTube — is technically coherent and strategically defensible. The architecture (Elixir/OTP + Cowboy on Hetzner, in-process Tokio Rust KV, OpenHuman-Protocol with delta motion + anti-crawl, OmniVoice-then-custom TTS, MetaHuman Animator pipeline) is unusually well-reasoned for a seed-stage company.

**Complication.** The plan asks a [ASSUMPTION: 3–8 person] team to ship six independently hard systems — Crawler, Animator, Motion model, Voice model, Protocol, Render Engine on three platforms — in roughly twelve months, before any external proof point exists. Phase 5 alone (custom WebGL2 + Vulkan + Metal render engines, no three.js) is a 12–18 month effort for a team of five specialists. The current Phase 1–8 sequencing implies a 16–24 month critical path before public launch. At seed-stage burn, that runway does not exist. The existential risk is not technical failure on any one module — it is parallel mediocrity across all of them, with nothing shippable when the next round needs to be raised.

**Resolution.** Compress to a single 6-month proof point: an end-to-end demo on **web only** (custom WebGL2), powered by OmniVoice + a pilot OpenHuman-Motion model trained on ~500 hours of data, served via Cowboy WebSocket from one Hetzner box behind Cloudflare. Defer iOS/Android render engines, the Phase 2 custom TTS, the Avatar Generator, and protocol open-sourcing. Use the demo to (a) validate latency and cache economics in production, (b) close 3–5 SDK pilots, and (c) raise Series A on a working artifact rather than a deck. **Option B (Balanced — Demo-led)** is the recommendation.

## 2. Current State Assessment

**Strengths to build on.** The cost architecture is the genuine moat — in-process Tokio Rust KV via NIF, narrow scope, and aggressive pre-compute give a structural cost advantage that HeyGen, Synthesia, and any incumbent on Kubernetes + Redis cannot replicate without rebuilding their stack. The TrueDepth synthesis plugin removes the iPhone bottleneck on data acquisition, which is the single highest-leverage decision in the document. The choice of Pixar style (avoids uncanny valley, lower mesh complexity, no biometric exposure) is the right product bet for the stated user segment.

**Vulnerabilities that are strategically material.** First, scope. Six modules, three render targets, two model training efforts, and a protocol spec is roughly 4× what a [ASSUMPTION: ≤8-engineer] team can ship to production quality in a funding window. Second, no demand validation. The thesis that elderly and blue-collar users want a Pixar character is plausible but unproven; no user research is referenced. Third, the data MOAT is contingent on legal posture — large-scale yt-dlp extraction for commercial model training sits in unsettled copyright territory, and YouTube's ToS prohibits it. This is not hypothetical risk; it is litigation exposure that gates institutional fundraising. Fourth, the anti-crawl protocol provides a cost barrier, not security; the document correctly notes this, but the strategic weight placed on format-MOAT may be overstated.

**The one thing that could kill OpenHuman.** Running out of cash with six 70%-complete modules and no demonstrable end-to-end product. This is the dominant failure mode for vertically integrated infrastructure plays at seed stage. It is more dangerous than any competitor.

## 3. Strategic Options

### Option A — Conservative: Data MOAT First

- **Core hypothesis:** The dataset is the only durable moat; build it to 10k+ hours before touching product, then sell or license access while training internal models.
- **12-month outcome:** 5–10k hours of paired (audio, transcript, 135 curves) data; one OpenHuman-Motion baseline checkpoint; no shippable product; possible academic/research licensing revenue <$200k.
- **Capital:** ~$1.5–2.5M (compute for MetaHuman Animator batch + storage + 2–3 ML engineers).
- **Timeline:** M1–3 pipeline; M3–9 extraction at scale; M9–12 model training. No demo until M14+.
- **Top risks:** (1) YouTube ToS / copyright challenge invalidates the dataset's commercial use — mitigation: parallel licensing deals with creator networks. (2) No external proof point for Series A — mitigation: publish dataset benchmarks and one open paper.
- **Kill condition:** A competitor (Meta, ByteDance) releases a comparable open dataset before M9.

### Option B — Balanced: Demo-Led (recommended)

- **Core hypothesis:** A working end-to-end demo on web — even with placeholder models — is worth more than any single completed module, because it unlocks pilots, fundraising, and real cache-hit data.
- **12-month outcome:** Live demo at openhuman.[tld] with OmniVoice + pilot Motion model; 3–5 signed B2B SDK pilots; closed beta of 1k–10k users; measured cache hit rate, time-to-first-byte, and cost-per-session in production; Series A closed on traction.
- **Capital:** ~$2–3M (1 Hetzner box scaling to a small cluster, Cloudflare, 4–6 engineers, ~500 hours of data extraction).
- **Timeline:** M1–2 Cowboy + Protocol v0 + WebGL2 skeleton; M3–4 OmniVoice integration + pilot Motion checkpoint; M5–6 closed beta + first pilots; M7–9 cache optimization to >50% hit rate; M10–12 Series A.
- **Top risks:** (1) Pilot Motion model quality is below the uncanny-valley threshold for Pixar (which is more forgiving but not zero) — mitigation: hand-animate the top 200 cached responses, ship those as "premium-quality cached" while the model improves. (2) Web-only excludes the kiosk/mobile use cases central to the elderly/blue-collar thesis — mitigation: explicitly frame web-first as B2B SDK validation, with mobile in the next phase.
- **Kill condition:** By M6, no pilot partner signs a paid commitment after demo.

### Option C — Aggressive: Protocol-Standard First

- **Core hypothesis:** Open-source the render SDK and publish OpenHuman-Protocol immediately; win the format war before the model war, on the MP3/WebRTC analogy.
- **12-month outcome:** Public protocol spec, open-source render engine on GitHub, possible community traction; no production-grade backend, no proprietary models shipped; competitors implement the spec before OpenHuman does.
- **Capital:** ~$1–1.5M (heavy on developer relations, light on infrastructure).
- **Timeline:** M1–4 protocol + render engine open-sourced; M4–8 community building; M8–12 commercial backend.
- **Top risks:** (1) Publishing the protocol exposes the anti-crawl design before anyone is using it, neutralizing it — mitigation: publish a v1 without the anti-crawl layer, keep that proprietary. (2) An incumbent (Epic, Meta) forks the protocol and ships a better implementation — mitigation: none reliable; this is the structural risk of going-first without scale.
- **Kill condition:** <500 GitHub stars and zero independent implementations within 6 months of publication.

---

## 4. Recommendation

**Choose Option B — Demo-Led.** A working end-to-end web demo on OmniVoice + a pilot OpenHuman-Motion model is the highest-information artifact this team can produce in 6 months, and it is the only path that simultaneously de-risks the technical thesis, validates demand, and produces fundraising leverage. Option A defers all market signal until cash is gone. Option C gives away the standard before the team can defend it. Demo-led keeps the data pipeline running in parallel at reduced intensity (~500 hours, not 10k), uses OmniVoice to skip the Voice training risk entirely in year one, and ships on web only — deferring the three render-engine builds that are the largest single source of scope inflation.

**The single most important assumption that must hold:** the pilot Motion model, trained on ~500 hours of MetaHuman Animator output, is good enough that a Pixar character does not look broken. If that fails, fall back to handcrafted motion for the top 200 cached responses and ship anyway.

## 5. 90-Day Priority Initiatives

1. **Ship Cowboy WebSocket + OpenHuman-Protocol v0 on one Hetzner box behind Cloudflare.** Owner: Backend lead. Metric: <500ms TTFB end-to-end with stub motion stream. Done = public demo URL with a placeholder character lip-syncing OmniVoice audio.
2. **Train pilot OpenHuman-Motion model on 500 hours of extracted data.** Owner: ML lead. Metric: lip-sync accuracy ≥ baseline + naturalness MOS ≥3.5 on 50 held-out clips. Done = checkpoint deployed behind the Cowboy GPU worker pool.
3. **Resolve YouTube data legal posture.** Owner: CEO + outside counsel. Metric: written counsel opinion on commercial training use. Done = either a green-light memo or signed licensing agreements with 2+ creator networks covering ≥1,000 hours.
4. **Land 2 signed LOIs from B2B SDK design partners.** Owner: CEO. Metric: LOIs from kiosk operator, telehealth platform, or accessibility-focused app. Done = countersigned with pilot scope and success criteria.
5. **Stand up OpenHuman-Cache (Tokio Rust NIF) and instrument hit rate from day one.** Owner: Infra engineer. Metric: <100µs p99 lookup; baseline hit rate measured. Done = live dashboard showing per-context-tree hit rate in production.

## 6. Resource Requirements

**People (immediate hires/redeploys, in priority order):**

- 1 senior ML engineer (motion modeling, PyTorch, diffusion/transformer experience).
- 1 senior Elixir/OTP engineer (Cowboy, NIFs, distribution).
- 1 senior Rust engineer for the Tokio KV NIF (can be contract for 3 months).
- 1 graphics engineer for the WebGL2 render core. **Defer iOS/Android hires until M9.**
- 1 part-time outside counsel for data licensing.
- **Defer:** dedicated BD, mobile engineers, kiosk/embedded engineers, Avatar Generator team.

**Capital:** $2–3M for 12 months. Allocation: ~45% engineering payroll, ~20% GPU compute (training + batch MetaHuman Animator), ~10% Hetzner + Cloudflare, ~10% data licensing buffer, ~15% reserve.

**Infrastructure (per stated constraints, non-negotiable):** Hetzner AMD EPYC, Cloudflare CDN/edge/DDoS, Postgres, custom Tokio Rust KV in-process via NIF, Cowboy/Elixir/OTP, custom WebGL2. No AWS/GCP/Azure, no Kubernetes, no Redis, no three.js.

**Strategy-breaker constraints to flag now:**

- A team smaller than 5 production engineers cannot execute Option B on this timeline. If headcount stays at 3, drop the custom Motion model in year one and ship with handcrafted motion plus OmniVoice — the demo still validates the protocol and cache thesis.
- GPU access on Hetzner is more constrained than on hyperscalers; secure capacity contracts now, not in M6.
- The data legal question is the single binary risk; do not raise Series A without a written legal position.

**Rule:** Default to Quadrant 1. Quadrant 2 is acceptable when it directly reduces an infrastructure cost line. Quadrant 4 is the most dangerous because it looks like growth but erodes the cost MOAT before it is proven. The Avatar Generator, multi-character, and multi-language expansion all live in Q3/Q4 — defer until cache hit rate sustainably exceeds 70% in production.

## 8. "If I Had One Hour" Brief

The most important insight: OpenHuman's true MOAT is not the dataset, the protocol, or the render engine — it is the **in-process Tokio Rust KV NIF inside Cowboy**, because it is the only architectural choice in this entire document that competitors structurally cannot copy without rewriting their stack. Everything else is defensible-with-effort; this one is defensible-by-architecture. The most important action is therefore to instrument cache hit rate and per-session cost from the day the first WebSocket connection lands, and treat those two numbers as the company's primary KPIs — above user count, above demo polish, above model quality. If cache hit rate exceeds 70% in closed beta on real traffic, every other strategic question becomes easier. If it does not, no amount of model quality saves the unit economics, and the entire free-tier-as-standard thesis collapses.
