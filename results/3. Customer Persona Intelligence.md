# OpenHuman — Customer Persona Intelligence

**Engagement type:** Pre-launch market entry analysis
**Scope:** Two-sided market — B2C end-users + B2B platform embedders
**Geographic anchor:** Vietnam-first → SEA → global free-tier scale
**MVP constraint envelope:** Single Pixar character · Vietnamese + English · <100 kbps · Free tier sustainable at 100M+ MAU
**Document version:** 1.0 — May 2026

---

## Executive Framing

OpenHuman is not a product looking for a market — it is a *protocol* looking for adoption. Personas must therefore be evaluated through two lenses simultaneously: **(a) who derives life-changing utility from the interface** (the demand side that creates pull), and **(b) who controls distribution surfaces where the interface gets embedded** (the supply side that creates ubiquity). A persona that scores high on emotional pull but low on distribution leverage will not produce a standard. A persona with distribution leverage but no emotional pull will produce a forgotten SDK. The four personas below are chosen specifically because each occupies a distinct quadrant of the pull × leverage matrix.

A second framing constraint: every persona analysis is grounded in the *digital human interface category*, not in generic SaaS or generic AI assistants. The question is never "would this persona adopt AI" — it is "would this persona prefer talking to a Pixar character over typing, calling, or visiting in person." That is a substantially narrower and more behaviorally-loaded question.

# B2C PERSONA 1 — "Bà Hạnh"

## The Excluded Majority

> *Strategic summary: A 64-year-old grandmother in Đà Nẵng who has a smartphone but uses ~3 apps; represents the 80% of humanity AI has never reached, and the single most defensible emotional moat OpenHuman can build.*

### 1. Profile Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 58–72 |
| **Income tier** | Lower-middle (₫6–12M / month, often pension + family transfers) |
| **Digital literacy** | Tier 1 — can answer Zalo calls, tap photos, scroll Facebook. Cannot type fluently in Vietnamese, cannot copy/paste, has never installed an app herself. |
| **Geography** | Tier-2 Vietnamese city (Đà Nẵng, Cần Thơ, Hải Phòng) — also extends to SEA equivalents (Indonesia, Philippines, Thailand) |
| **Household role** | Primary caregiver for grandchildren; secondary financial decision-maker |

### 2. Psychographic & Behavioral DNA

Bà Hạnh's relationship with technology is mediated entirely through *trusted humans*. Her son sets up her phone. Her daughter-in-law shows her how to scan a QR code. Her grandchild teaches her TikTok one weekend per year. **She does not learn from documentation, tutorials, or popups — she learns from people.** This single fact dictates her entire adoption pattern.

Her trust signals are face-based and voice-based, not interface-based. She does not read app store ratings; she trusts what her son installs. She does not trust text from unknown numbers but will answer any video call. **A Pixar character that talks back to her occupies the same trust slot as a video call from a real person** — it triggers the social-presence brain circuit, not the tool-use brain circuit. This is the single most important psychographic insight for OpenHuman's B2C strategy.

Her risk appetite toward AI is not skeptical so much as *unaware*. She has heard "ChatGPT" as a word but has no model of what it is. She does not fear AI taking jobs (she is past the labor market) and she does not fear data leaks (she has nothing she considers worth stealing). What she does fear is **embarrassment** — being seen by her grandchildren as old, slow, or incompetent. The dominant identity threat at her life stage is dignity loss, not privacy loss.

Her preference ordering across interfaces is unambiguous: **face-to-face > video call > voice call > visual character > photo + voice > voice-only > text.** A Pixar digital human sits *exactly* one notch below a video call — far above ChatGPT Voice in her implicit hierarchy, because the visual presence completes the social schema her brain expects.

### 3. Pain Architecture

| Rank | Pain | Stated vs. Latent | Frequency | Emotional intensity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "I can't read the small text on government and bank apps" | Stated | Daily | High — produces shame |
| 2 | "I have to wait for my son to come home to do anything online" | Stated as inconvenience | Weekly | Very high — **latent pain is loss of autonomy** |
| 3 | "I'm scared I'll press the wrong button and lose money" | Stated | Continuous background anxiety | Very high — produces avoidance |
| 4 | "I feel left behind from how the world works now" | Almost never stated aloud | Continuous | Severe — **the deepest latent pain, rarely surfaced** |
| 5 | "I miss talking to people during the day" | Stated to family, dismissed by them | Daily | Moderate — quietly compounding |

The strategic insight: pains 4 and 5 are **larger than 1–3 combined**, but they are invisible in surveys because Bà Hạnh will never volunteer them. She will say "I just need bigger fonts." OpenHuman that solves only pain 1 is a font setting. OpenHuman that solves pains 4 and 5 is a category-defining product. **The Pixar character is uniquely positioned to solve pains 4 and 5 because it provides social presence as a side effect of solving pain 1.**

### 4. Goals & Success Metrics (6–12 months)

Functional outcome: Bà Hạnh independently completes at least three tasks per week that previously required her son — checking pension status, paying utility bills, looking up a medication, calling a clinic. Emotional outcome — and this is what determines retention — she begins to refer to the character by name in conversation with family ("I asked Linh and she told me…"), indicating the character has crossed from *tool* into *social object*. When Bà Hạnh says "Linh told me," OpenHuman has won her segment for life. That phrase is the leading indicator; bill-payment count is the lagging indicator.

### 5. Adoption Journey Map

**Awareness:** Almost never via her own discovery. The vector is her son, daughter, or grandchild showing it to her — usually at a family meal, on someone else's phone first. The "wow moment" is hearing the character speak Vietnamese with natural prosody, *not* the visual fidelity. **Friction:** zero, if the family member sets it up.

**First contact:** The first 60 seconds determine everything. If the character greets her by name (provided by family), in her dialect, with eye contact and a smile — she will keep talking. If it pauses, mispronounces, or asks her to type anything — she will hand the phone back and never return. **Friction unique to visual AI:** the uncanny valley risk is paradoxically *higher* for elderly Vietnamese users than for tech-savvy users, because their reference frame is real human conversation, not other AI products. Pixar style is therefore not stylistic preference but a *mandatory* design decision for this persona.

**Trust threshold:** Crossed when the character successfully helps with one real task involving money — typically a bill payment or pension check. Until money is involved, it's a toy. Once money flows correctly, the character is real. **Friction:** if any task fails publicly in front of family, trust resets to zero. **First-task reliability matters more than feature breadth.**

**Habitual use:** 30–50 daily turns once trust is established, mostly low-stakes companionship interleaved with occasional high-stakes tasks. The 50:1 ratio of chitchat to task is itself the business model — chitchat is high-cache-hit, near-zero-marginal-cost, but produces the engagement that makes tasks possible.

**Advocacy:** She will not post on social media. She *will* tell every friend in her morning market group, her temple group, and her WhatsApp/Zalo family chat — and she will physically show them on her phone. **Word-of-mouth in her cohort is offline and dense; one Bà Hạnh recruits 5–15 peers within 3 months if she is genuinely retained.**

**Gatekeepers and influencers:** The son or daughter-in-law is the installation gatekeeper (must approve and install). The grandchild is the demonstration influencer ("look grandma, watch this"). The peer group at the morning market is the validation network. OpenHuman must design for all three audiences — the family member's setup flow, the grandchild's "show off" moment, and the peer-to-peer demo.

### 6. Media & Channel Consumption

**Zalo:** ~2–4 hours/day, primarily voice messages and family group chats. Zalo is her dominant communication surface and the **single highest-priority embedding partner** for this persona — if OpenHuman is reachable as a Zalo Mini App or Zalo bot, distribution is solved.

**Facebook:** ~1–2 hours/day, video feed scrolling, family photos, occasional shares of motivational content and folk medicine remedies. Facebook is the discovery surface, not the engagement surface.

**YouTube:** ~1 hour/day, primarily Vietnamese folk music, Buddhist teachings, cooking, traditional medicine. YouTube ads in Vietnamese with a real human face are her highest-recall ad format.

**TikTok:** Lower usage in this cohort than younger users (~20–40 min/day), but rising fast. Notable: short-form video of "grandma talks to AI character and laughs" is *exactly* the viral format that recruits this persona at scale.

**Offline:** The morning wet market (5:30–7:00 AM social hub), the temple/pagoda, family meals, the local pharmacy. **The pharmacy and the local clinic waiting room are critically underexploited B2C acquisition surfaces** — a kiosk or tablet running OpenHuman in those locations would convert at extraordinary rates.

### 7. Objection Register

> **Objection 1:** *"Nó có hiểu tiếng tôi nói không? Tôi nói giọng miền Trung."* ("Will it understand how I talk? I have a central-region accent.")
>
> **Counter-narrative:** OpenHuman's Vietnamese is trained on YouTube data spanning all major Vietnamese dialects (northern, central, southern), specifically including content from regional creators. Demonstrate live with her own accent in the first 30 seconds — do not explain, demonstrate. The MVP's narrow language pair scope is the *enabler* of dialect coverage, not a limitation.

> **Objection 2:** *"Nhỡ tôi bấm nhầm thì sao? Tôi sợ mất tiền."* ("What if I press the wrong thing? I'm scared of losing money.")
>
> **Counter-narrative:** OpenHuman's primary interface is voice — there is no "wrong button" because there are essentially no buttons. Critically, the character *confirms* every money-related action verbally before executing, in the same way her son currently does. The confirmation pattern matches her existing trusted-human protocol. Pair with a one-tap "undo" that her grandchild also has access to from their device via cross-device memory.

> **Objection 3:** *"Cái này có phải lừa đảo không? Trên TV nói nhiều vụ AI lừa người già."* ("Is this a scam? TV reports a lot of AI scams against elderly.")
>
> **Counter-narrative:** This is the most strategically important objection because it is rising fast and will dominate by 2027. The counter must be structural, not promotional. **Pixar style is itself the answer**: the character is *visibly* a cartoon, not a fake person — it cannot be confused with a deepfake of a relative because it does not pretend to be human. Pair with: distribution through trusted brands (Vietcombank, FPT, hospital partnerships) so the character appears in contexts she already trusts, not as an unknown app.

### 8. Trigger Events

A failed interaction with VssID (Vietnamese social insurance app), a confusing IVR loop with a state-owned bank, a hospital appointment booking that requires a national ID upload she cannot perform, or a moment of social humiliation when she cannot pay digitally at a market stall that has gone cashless. Any of these events, occurring within 48 hours of family exposure to OpenHuman, produces near-certain conversion. **Behavioral signal OpenHuman can monitor:** family-member-initiated installs spike around national policy changes (e.g., mandatory VNeID adoption, BHXH digitization) — these are pre-announced and OpenHuman should pre-position campaigns 30–60 days ahead.

### 9. Willingness-to-Pay Analysis

Direct WTP from Bà Hạnh: effectively zero. She will not enter a credit card, will not authorize a recurring charge, will not understand a paywall. **This is not a bug — it is the core strategic reason OpenHuman must be free at this tier.**

Indirect WTP via family: substantial. Her son will pay $3–8/month for a "premium grandma plan" that includes cross-device memory (so he can see what she asked about), priority response, and a panic-handoff feature where the character escalates to him on detected confusion or distress. **The unit economics of this segment are not Bà Hạnh's wallet — they are her son's peace of mind.** Privacy/data trade-offs: she will accept all of them implicitly, which is precisely why OpenHuman must adopt stricter-than-required defaults on her behalf. The Pixar/no-face-capture stance is not just a moat — it is an ethical floor for serving this persona.

# B2C PERSONA 2 — "Anh Tuấn"

## The Pragmatic Operator

> *Strategic summary: A 38-year-old factory line supervisor in Bình Dương who can install apps but cannot prompt-engineer, representing the working-class adopter who converts AI from elite tool to mass infrastructure.*

### 1. Profile Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 32–48 |
| **Income tier** | ₫12–22M / month — solidly working-class, often supporting parents and 1–2 children |
| **Digital literacy** | Tier 2 — installs apps, uses banking, watches YouTube tutorials, sends voice messages on Zalo. Has *tried* ChatGPT once, did not return. |
| **Geography** | Industrial-park belt — Bình Dương, Đồng Nai, Bắc Ninh, Hải Phòng. Generalizes to manufacturing belts in Indonesia, Bangladesh, Mexico. |
| **Household role** | Sole or primary breadwinner; technical decision-maker for household digital purchases |

### 2. Psychographic & Behavioral DNA

Anh Tuấn is the most underestimated AI consumer in the world. He is fully digitally native in *transactional* terms — he banks, shops, navigates, communicates — but he has been excluded from the AI productivity revolution because that revolution was designed in English, around text input, with assumed familiarity with a chat-completion paradigm. **He represents roughly 1.5 billion people globally and is functionally invisible in Silicon Valley product roadmaps.**

His values cluster around *competence and provision*: being good at his job, being respected by his team, providing for his family, learning practical skills (English for promotion, motorcycle repair, real estate basics, stock trading basics). He is the persona most likely to use OpenHuman as a **coach** — for English practice, for skill-up content, for second-opinion advice on contracts and household financial decisions.

His trust calibration toward AI is mid-skeptical but practically-oriented. He does not believe AI will solve everything; he wants to know if it will help *this specific task right now*. The tolerance window for OpenHuman is short and outcome-based: solve a real problem in the first session or lose him for 6 months.

His interface preferences are revealing: **voice input >> text input, but visual confirmation matters.** He will speak commands in noisy factory environments only if the character's response is visually scannable in 1–2 seconds (he cannot wear earbuds during shifts). The Pixar character is therefore not just emotional dressing — it is a *performance feature* that lets him glance and parse.

### 3. Pain Architecture

| Rank | Pain | Stated vs. Latent | Frequency | Emotional intensity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "I want to learn English to get promoted but classes are expensive and I'm too tired after shifts" | Stated | Daily aspiration | High |
| 2 | "I can't get a straight answer on legal/financial questions without paying a lawyer" | Stated | Monthly | High — affects family wealth |
| 3 | "ChatGPT is too complicated and the Vietnamese is bad" | Stated | When tried | Moderate, dismissive |
| 4 | "My English is bad enough that I'm embarrassed to practice with real people" | Latent — surfaced only after rapport | Continuous | Very high |
| 5 | "I want my kids to see I'm learning and modern, not stuck behind" | Latent — almost never stated | Continuous | Severe — identity-level |

Pain 4 is the single most monetizable pain in this segment globally. Embarrassment-free English practice with a non-judgmental visual partner is a category that has been attempted (Duolingo, Cake, ELSA) but no product has cracked the *visual conversational* dimension at the price point this persona requires (free or near-free). **This is OpenHuman's clearest direct-consumer wedge.**

### 4. Goals & Success Metrics (6–12 months)

Functional: 15–30 minutes of daily English conversation with the character, measurable improvement in workplace English (rated by self or supervisor), and 2–3 successful "second opinion" sessions on real-life decisions (a contract clause, a bank product, a school choice for his child). Emotional: he begins recommending OpenHuman to coworkers during smoke breaks, framed as "this is how I'm leveling up." That framing — leveling up, not "AI" — is the leading indicator of segment fit.

### 5. Adoption Journey Map

**Awareness:** Facebook reels and TikTok in Vietnamese. The hook that works is *"công nhân Việt Nam học tiếng Anh với AI miễn phí"* — Vietnamese workers learning English with free AI. The hook that fails is anything mentioning "ChatGPT alternative" — that frame loses him because he has already filed ChatGPT under "not for me." **Friction:** ad creative must star a person who looks like him, not a model in business attire.

**First contact:** Downloads on his own phone after seeing a peer use it. The first session must produce one tangible English correction or one piece of useful information he didn't have before. **Friction unique to visual AI:** he will instinctively talk to it like he talks to a YouTube video — observing, not interacting. The character must invite him into conversation in the first 10 seconds, in his language, with a question he wants to answer.

**Trust threshold:** Crossed when he uses the character in front of his family or coworkers without embarrassment — meaning the character is good enough that *its existence does not reflect badly on him*. This is a subtle but critical bar. A clunky AI makes him look foolish; a polished Pixar character makes him look forward-thinking.

**Habitual use:** 20–40 minutes/day, time-shifted to commute, lunch break, and post-shift. Heavy use of background voice mode while doing other tasks — a workload pattern that uniquely benefits from OpenHuman's audio-first streaming with optional visual.

**Advocacy:** Active in factory-worker Facebook groups and Zalo group chats. **Vietnamese factory worker Facebook groups (50k–500k members each) are the highest-velocity B2C acquisition channel for this persona.** One viral post from a credible peer recruits 1,000–10,000 installs in 72 hours.

**Gatekeepers:** None institutionally — he installs his own apps. Influencers: factory floor opinion leaders (the older worker who's already passed his English certification), TikTok creators in the công nhân (worker) genre, and his teenage child who validates whether the app is "real or scammy."

### 6. Media & Channel Consumption

**Facebook:** 2–3 hours/day, heavy reels consumption, factory-worker community groups, marketplace.
**TikTok:** 1–2 hours/day, primary discovery surface for new apps and skills.
**YouTube:** ~1 hour/day, English learning, motorcycle/repair, financial literacy, real estate.
**Zalo:** Communications backbone.
**Offline:** Factory floor (peer recommendations during breaks), motorcycle parking lot (longest social interaction surface of the day), evening street food stalls, weekend community centers.

### 7. Objection Register

> **Objection 1:** *"Tôi đã thử ChatGPT rồi, tiếng Việt nó dở ẹc."* ("I tried ChatGPT, its Vietnamese is terrible.")
>
> **Counter-narrative:** OpenHuman is not a ChatGPT wrapper. The Vietnamese is trained from Vietnamese video data, not translated from English models. Demonstrate by having him speak naturally, including code-switching ("Sếp tôi nói deadline rồi mà parts chưa về") — and have the character respond in matching register. The single demo beats any marketing claim.

> **Objection 2:** *"Cái này có tốn data không? Tôi không có wifi ở phòng trọ."* ("Will this burn my data? I don't have wifi in my rented room.")
>
> **Counter-narrative:** <100 kbps bandwidth = roughly 45 MB/hour of conversation. A typical worker's data plan handles 10+ hours/day at this rate. **This is exactly where OpenHuman's <100 kbps technical decision pays a marketing dividend** — frame the bandwidth efficiency as "rẻ hơn xem TikTok" (cheaper than TikTok scrolling), which is concretely true.

> **Objection 3:** *"Tôi ngại nói tiếng Anh sai trước máy nó ghi lại."* ("I'm embarrassed to speak wrong English while it records me.")
>
> **Counter-narrative:** OpenHuman's privacy controls allow him to delete any session. The character does not record audio — it processes mel-spectrograms and discards. Critically: the Pixar character is *designed not to judge* — it does not have the human cues that trigger embarrassment. He can practice with it the way a child practices with a stuffed animal — protected by the obvious unreality of the partner. This is a place where the Pixar choice is again a *feature*, not aesthetic.

### 8. Trigger Events

A failed promotion interview where English was the gap. A pay raise that creates 30–60 days of optimism and self-improvement appetite. A factory English-language training program announcement (which he wants to prepare for in advance to avoid embarrassment). A child entering an English-required school year. **Behavioral signal:** spikes in TikTok English-learning content saves correlate to acquisition windows; OpenHuman can monitor TikTok hashtag velocity in Vietnamese (#hocTiengAnh, #congnhanhocAnh) as a real-time demand signal.

### 9. Willingness-to-Pay Analysis

Direct WTP: ₫50–150k/month (~$2–6) for a clear premium feature set. The price ceiling is set by the local Duolingo Plus and Cake premium tiers, both of which he knows. **Premium feature anchors that justify pay:** (1) unlimited English practice with prosody feedback, (2) cross-device memory so he can pick up exactly where he left off between phone and a future workplace tablet, (3) "career mode" with curated workplace English scenarios. He is privacy-aware but pragmatic — he will accept usage data sharing for a free tier and pay a small premium for explicit privacy controls.

# B2B PERSONA 3 — "Linh, Head of Digital Banking, Vietnamese Tier-2 Bank"

## The Regulated Embedder

> *Strategic summary: A 41-year-old digital banking executive at a top-10 Vietnamese bank under SBV pressure to serve unbanked elderly customers, representing the highest-leverage embedding partner in the MVP geography because she controls a distribution surface used daily by the persona OpenHuman most needs to reach.*

### 1. Profile Overview

| Attribute | Value |
|---|---|
| **Age** | 36–48 |
| **Job function** | Head of Digital Banking / Director of Customer Experience / Chief Innovation Officer at a Vietnamese commercial bank |
| **Organization size** | Tier 1–2 Vietnamese bank (Vietcombank, BIDV, Techcombank, MB, ACB scale, or top regional) — 5M–25M customers |
| **Budget authority** | $500k–$3M annual innovation budget; can authorize pilots up to $200k unilaterally |
| **Geography** | Hanoi or HCMC HQ, with branch network across Vietnam |

### 2. Psychographic & Behavioral DNA

Linh operates inside three simultaneous pressures that make her the single most valuable B2B target in the MVP market: (1) **regulatory pressure** from SBV (State Bank of Vietnam) and the broader push toward financial inclusion, including digital banking access for elderly and rural customers; (2) **competitive pressure** from VPBank's Cake and MB's MBBank Plus, which have moved earlier on conversational interfaces; (3) **internal pressure** from her CEO to show "AI-driven innovation" in the next annual report. She does not need to be sold on the *category* — she is already shopping. She needs to be sold on the *specific solution* and, critically, on the *risk profile*.

Her trust signals are structural, not pitch-based. She trusts: vendor financial stability, on-premise / sovereign-cloud deployment options, signed customer references in regulated industries, and certifications (ISO 27001, SOC 2, PCI DSS where relevant). She distrusts: GitHub-only documentation, "open-source means free" framing without enterprise SLA, and US-centric products that assume English-default.

Her risk appetite is asymmetric. She gains career upside from a successful innovation but faces severe career downside from a public failure (deepfake incident, customer harm, regulatory censure). **The Pixar style is therefore not a stylistic choice from her perspective — it is a critical risk-control feature.** A non-realistic character cannot be confused with a real bank employee, cannot impersonate, cannot deepfake. This argument lands with her in a way it does not land with a B2C user.

### 3. Pain Architecture

| Rank | Pain | Stated vs. Latent | Frequency | Emotional/business intensity |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| 1 | "Our IVR call center costs $X per call and elderly customers still can't navigate it" | Stated, with exact numbers | Continuous | Very high — direct P&L hit |
| 2 | "Branch foot traffic from elderly customers is rising while we close branches; we can't serve both economics" | Stated to executives | Continuous | Very high — strategic |
| 3 | "Our existing chatbot has 12% containment rate; customers escape to human agents" | Stated, embarrassedly | Continuous | High — visible KPI miss |
| 4 | "Integration risk: a vendor pilot that fails publicly damages my career, not just the project" | Latent, never said | Continuous | Severe — drives all decisions |
| 5 | "We are perceived internally as slow on AI compared to VPBank/Techcombank" | Latent, political | Continuous | High — promotion-relevant |

The strategic insight: pain 4 is the *governing* pain for this persona, even though pains 1–3 are the ones that justify the budget. The OpenHuman pitch must lead with capability *and* close with risk-mitigation: pilot scope contained, on-premise deployment available, no customer face data collected, character is visibly non-human, full audit logs, kill switch.

### 4. Goals & Success Metrics

In 6 months: a public pilot announcement that lands in Vietnamese tech press, 20–40% containment-rate improvement on a defined customer segment (elderly mobile banking), and a measurable reduction in IVR cost-per-resolved-issue. In 12 months: rollout to her full digital channel with documented NPS lift among 60+ customers and a case study suitable for the bank's annual report. **Her personal success metric:** being quoted by name in the announcement and presenting the case study at FinanceAsia or similar regional conference.

Concrete KPIs she will track:
- Containment rate (target lift: 35% → 65%+ for elderly segment)
- Cost-per-resolved-interaction (target: 60–80% reduction vs. IVR/agent)
- NPS among customers aged 55+ (target: +15 points)
- Mobile app DAU among elderly cohort (target: 2x within 6 months)
- Compliance incident count (target: zero — non-negotiable)

### 5. Adoption Journey Map

**Awareness:** Industry conferences (FinanceAsia, Vietnam Banking Forum), peer CEO conversations, board-level AI urgency cascades, McKinsey/BCG reports landing on her CEO's desk. **Friction:** OpenHuman is unknown — credibility must be borrowed via lighthouse logos, regulatory endorsements, or government-affiliated pilots.

**First contact:** Inbound from her innovation team after a sales engineer (or a viral demo) lands. The decision to take the meeting is made by her team lead; the decision to greenlight a pilot is made by her after a 30–60 day evaluation. **Friction unique to visual AI:** her legal and risk teams will raise concerns OpenHuman has likely never heard before — voice cloning policy, biometric data classification under Vietnam's Decree 13/2023 (Personal Data Protection), liability for character-given financial advice. OpenHuman must arrive with a pre-built compliance binder, not assemble one in response.

**Trust threshold:** Crossed only after a closed pilot (typically 1,000–5,000 customers, internal staff first) demonstrates zero compliance incidents and measurable KPI lift over 60–90 days. **No marketing material substitutes for this milestone.**

**Habitual use:** Once embedded, the digital human becomes the front door for 30–60% of her bank's elderly customer interactions within 18 months. The integration is sticky because (a) regulatory approval is per-vendor and re-doing it is costly, (b) the bank's brand becomes associated with the character voice, and (c) cross-device memory creates customer-side switching costs.

**Advocacy:** She will present the case study at conferences, write a LinkedIn post that gets shared by peers, and refer counterparts at other banks. **One Linh successfully landed produces 3–7 inbound qualified leads from peer banks within 12 months — this is the MOAT-formation mechanism for B2B.**

**Gatekeepers and influencers:** CIO/CTO (technical approval), CRO/Head of Risk (compliance approval), Head of Legal (Decree 13/2023 sign-off), CEO (budget and PR approval). The CTO is often the *anti-gatekeeper* — junior engineers love the tech but the CTO blocks it on integration concerns. OpenHuman's response: ship a reference implementation with one specific Vietnamese core banking system (T24, Flexcube) pre-integrated.

### 6. Media & Channel Consumption

LinkedIn (heavy — primary professional channel), Vietnam tech press (CafeF, VnExpress Kinh doanh, ICTNews), regional banking publications (The Asian Banker, FinanceAsia), McKinsey and BCG digital banking reports, Vietnam-specific WhatsApp/Zalo executive groups. **GitHub and Hacker News are essentially zero for her** — these channels reach her engineers but not her. Conferences: SBV-sponsored events, Vietnam ICT Summit, Singapore Fintech Festival.

### 7. Objection Register

> **Objection 1:** *"How do we ensure customers don't think they're talking to a real bank employee and rely on the character's responses as binding advice?"*
>
> **Counter-narrative:** This is precisely why OpenHuman is Pixar-stylized rather than realistic. Visual non-humanness is a *design feature for regulated industries* — the character cannot be confused with a real employee, cannot impersonate, cannot create false authority. Pair with: every financial-advice-adjacent response includes a disclosed escalation path to a human banker, configurable by Linh's compliance team. The character knows what it doesn't know.

> **Objection 2:** *"What's our exposure if your model goes down? We can't have a dead character on our flagship app."*
>
> **Counter-narrative:** OpenHuman supports on-premise or sovereign-cloud deployment of the inference layer for tier-1 enterprise customers, with bank-controlled failover to graceful text-mode degradation. The Cowboy/Erlang OTP architecture is fault-tolerant by construction (Discord runs on the same stack at scale). Pair with: 99.95% SLA, contractual penalty clauses, and a documented failover playbook.

> **Objection 3:** *"You're a startup with one character and one language. What happens when we want to expand to ASEAN markets?"*
>
> **Counter-narrative:** The MVP scope is intentional cost discipline, not capability ceiling. The motion model architecture is language-agnostic; voice and ASR are the per-language work. The roadmap explicitly addresses regional expansion via partnership with each market's distribution leader (Linh's bank itself could be that partner if expanding into Cambodia, Laos). Frame the narrow MVP as the reason OpenHuman can serve Vietnam *deeply* rather than serving 40 countries shallowly.

### 8. Trigger Events

SBV publishes new financial inclusion guidelines (announced 30–90 days in advance). A competitor bank announces a conversational AI launch (creates 60-day window of CEO-level urgency). Quarterly earnings call where her bank's digital cost-per-customer metric underperforms peers. A public IVR-failure incident at a peer bank that goes viral. Her annual budget cycle (Vietnamese banks: typically Q4 budget for following calendar year). **Behavioral signal:** SBV consultation paper publications and press releases are pre-public on the SBV website 1–4 weeks before formal announcement; OpenHuman's Vietnam business development team should monitor weekly.

### 9. Willingness-to-Pay & Embed Analysis

Budget tier: enterprise license starts at $200k–$500k/year for tier-1 Vietnamese bank deployment, scaling to $1M+ at full footprint. Preferred model: **annual enterprise license with usage caps + per-million-interaction overage**, not pure usage-based (which makes her budget unpredictable). ## ① Segment Sizing

## ③ GTM Sequencing Recommendation

The sequencing must be designed so that each persona's adoption *enables* the next, rather than being pursued in parallel and starving each other.

**Stage 0 — Pre-MVP (now → 1k users):** Lead with **Marcus**. Open-source the protocol spec, the renderer, and a minimal working demo with the MVP character. Ship a "Show HN" post the moment the demo runs at <500ms first-byte on a mid-range device. The goal at this stage is not user count — it is *credibility currency* among Marcuses, measured in GitHub stars and meaningful issues.

**Stage 1 — MVP (1k → 10k users):** Layer in **Anh Tuấn** via TikTok/Facebook acquisition with the English-learning wedge. This persona is the highest-fit direct-consumer wedge because (a) he has direct WTP, (b) he is a noisy advocate within Vietnamese factory worker communities, and (c) his usage produces dense data signal in conversational Vietnamese that improves the model for the next stage. **Why not lead with Bà Hạnh:** acquisition is bottlenecked on Linh-class partners, who require the credibility OpenHuman doesn't yet have.

**Stage 2 — Beta (10k → 1M users):** Open the **Linh** funnel. By this point OpenHuman has: a working SDK Marcus has validated, a real Vietnamese B2C base demonstrating product-market fit, and conversational quality benchmarks. Now begin enterprise sales to one tier-1 Vietnamese bank and one healthcare partner. **The first Linh closes 9–12 months after MVP launch, not before.** Trying to close her earlier wastes scarce sales-engineering bandwidth on a deal that will fail at compliance review.

**Stage 3 — Scale (1M → 100M users):** Linh's deployment puts OpenHuman in front of **Bà Hạnh** at scale. She arrives via the bank app, the hospital kiosk, the government services portal — channels she already trusts. The viral peer-to-peer recruitment in her segment then compounds. Simultaneously, Marcus-tier adoption globally provides the embedded surfaces for non-Vietnamese expansion.

The sequencing logic in one sentence: **Marcus credibility → Anh Tuấn revenue + data → Linh distribution → Bà Hạnh moat → global standard.**

## ④ Cross-Segment Synergy Map

Several product features and messaging frames serve multiple personas simultaneously, and these are the highest-ROI investments because they amortize across segments.

**"Zero-typing required."** Serves Bà Hạnh (cannot type), Anh Tuấn (will not type during shift), Linh's customers (elderly banking customers), and is a meaningful differentiator for Marcus's accessibility-conscious products. Single feature, four-persona unlock.

**Pixar style as both aesthetic and risk control.** Reduces uncanny valley for B2C personas, reduces deepfake/impersonation liability for Linh, provides a unique visual identity for Marcus to differentiate his product. The single design decision compounds across all four personas in a way realistic-style or voice-only cannot.

**<100 kbps bandwidth.** Direct functional value for Anh Tuấn (cheap data plans), for Bà Hạnh (low-end devices), for Linh's rural customers, and for Marcus's mobile-first product economics. The technical decision is also the marketing decision.

**Cross-device memory.** Highest emotional value for Bà Hạnh's son (peace-of-mind premium), highest practical value for Anh Tuấn (continuity between devices), required feature for Linh's omnichannel banking, killer differentiator for Marcus's platform integrations. **This is the single most cross-cutting premium feature** and should be prioritized engineering-wise even at MVP.

**Open protocol + branded character.** Marcus adopts because the protocol is open; Linh pays because the character is branded. The combination is strategically beautiful: openness drives reach, branding drives revenue, neither cannibalizes the other.

**Vietnamese-first quality.** Direct value for Bà Hạnh and Anh Tuấn; necessary credibility for Linh ("this isn't just an English product translated"); a unique-in-market story for Marcus's Vietnamese-American or SEA-expansion use cases.

## ⑤ Standard Formation Signal

The single adoption milestone that most credibly signals OpenHuman is becoming the de-facto digital human standard is **a competitor-of-the-first-Linh adopting OpenHuman without OpenHuman pursuing them.**

Concretely: when the second Vietnamese tier-1 bank (or the first regional bank in Indonesia, Thailand, or Philippines) calls OpenHuman's sales line because their customers are asking for the character experience the first bank deployed, the inversion has occurred. Pursuit becomes pull. This is the equivalent of the moment Mapbox became default for mobile maps — not when Foursquare adopted them, but when a major airline embedded Mapbox without a Mapbox sales rep ever speaking to them, because the iOS developer had read the docs over a weekend.

A second, parallel signal worth monitoring: **a Marcus at a non-Vietnamese platform shipping a OpenHuman integration to production for a non-Vietnamese audience.** This signals the protocol has achieved credibility independent of the language pair, which is the precondition for global standard formation.

A third, weaker but earlier signal: **the protocol spec is independently re-implemented by a third party** (open-source clone, alternative renderer). Counter-intuitively, this is a *positive* signal — it indicates the protocol has crossed from product to infrastructure. Mapbox welcomed alternative styles built on Mapbox Style Spec for exactly this reason. OpenHuman should welcome alternative renderers built on OpenHuman-Protocol, and should anti-crawl only the *content* (motion + voice output of the trained models), not the *protocol* itself. This distinction is critical and should be made explicit in the public protocol spec.
