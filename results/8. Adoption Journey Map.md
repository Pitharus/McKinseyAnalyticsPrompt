# OpenHuman Adoption Journey Map

**Boardroom-Ready Strategic Document — Two-Track Lifecycle Analysis**

_Prepared for: OpenHuman Executive Team_
_Scope: Pre-launch MVP through Standard Formation (100M+ MAU)_
_Tracks: (A) End-User · (B) Platform Partner / Developer_

---

## Strategic Frame

OpenHuman is not a chatbot, not a voice assistant, and not a 3D avatar. It is a **streaming visual interlocutor** whose adoption mechanics most closely resemble three precedents:

- **Discord (2015–2018)** — proved that an Erlang/OTP stack can win on free-tier reliability and that "drop-in for any community" beats "best-in-class for one community."
- **Mapbox (2014–2019)** — proved that a custom render engine SDK becomes a defensible moat when third-party apps embed it as infrastructure.
- **WebRTC (2011–2017)** — proved that a streaming protocol becomes a standard only when the reference implementation is ubiquitous _before_ the spec is finalized.

The journey map below is structured to maximize the probability that OpenHuman replicates all three trajectories simultaneously. Where I rely on these analogies versus on direct evidence, I flag it explicitly.

A note on the two tracks: they are not parallel. Track B (partners) is the **multiplier** for Track A (end-users). A single Zoom integration delivers more end-users in one quarter than a year of direct consumer marketing. But Track B will not embed until Track A shows organic pull. This circular dependency is the central GTM problem, and it shapes every recommendation that follows.

## TRACK A — End-User Journey

_Persona: Low digital literacy. Has never knowingly used AI. May not type fluently. Mental model for "talking to a computer" is informed by sci-fi or by frustrating IVR phone menus, not by ChatGPT._

### Stage 1 — Awareness

**1. Reality on the ground.** A 62-year-old market vendor sees a niece using "the cartoon that talks back" on her phone. A factory worker watches a coworker ask the character about a medication during lunch break. The dominant thought is not _"what is this technology"_ but _"is that thing real or a recording, and is that person actually getting useful answers from it?"_ Sentiment: **5/10** — neutral curiosity tinged with the residual suspicion that anything labeled "AI" is either a scam or not for them. Critically, this user has no vocabulary for what they're seeing — they will describe it as "the cartoon" or "the talking face," never as "an AI" or "an assistant."

**2. Touchpoint inventory.** Owned: zero direct channels matter for this segment — they don't read tech press, don't follow product launches. Earned: word-of-mouth from family members (highest-trust channel for this demographic), short-form video (TikTok, YouTube Shorts, Facebook Reels) where someone records the character answering a question. Paid: pre-roll on family-oriented YouTube content, in-language radio spots. Physical: kiosks in pharmacies, bus terminals, government service centers, community health clinics. The kiosk channel is uniquely valuable here because it eliminates the install friction that would otherwise filter out the entire segment.

**3. Friction & failure modes.** **HIGH:** No mental model — user cannot even formulate a search query for the product because they don't know what to call it. Root cause: category novelty, not OpenHuman-specific. **HIGH:** Mistrust of AI — recent deepfake scams targeting elderly populations create active resistance. Root cause: external, but Pixar styling is the architectural mitigation. **MED:** Language/dialect mismatch — MVP is one language pair, but within that pair there are regional dialects the model may not handle. Root cause: data acquisition skew toward standard-dialect YouTube content.

**4. Delight opportunities.** The character waving and saying _the user's name in their language with correct local pronunciation_ during a kiosk demo. This is unachievable by a chatbot and emotionally distinct from voice-only — the visual recognition activates the same social circuitry as meeting a person who knows you. This single moment is the most word-of-mouth-generative event in the entire funnel for this demographic.

**5. Leading indicators.** (i) **Unaided category recall** in survey: % of target users who can describe "an AI character you can talk to" without prompting — benchmark <5% pre-launch, target 15% by month 12 in MVP market. (ii) **Kiosk dwell time** — median seconds a passerby spends watching the character before walking away — target 30s+ (Mapbox-analogous engagement benchmarks suggest 20–40s is the conversion threshold for novel visual experiences). (iii) **Branded search volume** in MVP language — target 50k/mo by month 12.

**6. Recommended interventions.** **Quick Win:** Deploy 50–100 demo kiosks in high-foot-traffic locations in one MVP city (pharmacies, community centers). Each kiosk runs entirely off pre-computed cache for the first 90 seconds — strengthens the cost MOAT because these high-volume demo interactions never touch GPU. **Strategic:** Partner with one trusted in-language media personality (not a tech influencer — a respected figure in the demographic, e.g., a beloved actor) for a sustained content series showing them having natural conversations with the character. This builds category vocabulary. **Strategic, flagged:** Every kiosk interaction is a data flywheel input — anonymized audio + transcript + (with consent) facial reactions improve OpenHuman-Animator's emotion detection. This compounds the data MOAT.

### Stage 2 — Consideration & Trust-Building

**1. Reality on the ground.** The user has now seen the character three or four times. A trusted person — usually a family member — has told them _"you can ask it anything, it's free, it won't judge you."_ The internal monologue shifts from _"what is this"_ to _"is this safe for me, specifically."_ Sentiment: **6/10**. The decisive question is not _"does it work"_ but _"will I be embarrassed when I try it."_ This is fundamentally different from how digitally-fluent users evaluate software, and missing this distinction is the single most common GTM mistake in this segment.

**2. Touchpoint inventory.** Earned: a family member literally handing the user a phone with the character already loaded — this is a sponsored installation, not a self-directed download. Owned: an extremely simple landing page in MVP language with one big button and a 15-second character video, no product copy. Physical: in-person demos at community centers, religious institutions (highly underrated trust channel), senior centers. Earned: peer testimony — videos of _people who look like the user_ having successful conversations.

**3. Friction & failure modes.** **HIGH:** Install friction — app store accounts, passwords, payment info screens (even on a free app) are blocking. Root cause: mobile OS UX is built for digitally-fluent users. Mitigation: web-first via the Render Engine's web component eliminates the install step entirely. **HIGH:** Fear of saying the wrong thing — user worries the character will respond in a way that confuses them or makes them feel stupid. Root cause: prior bad experiences with IVR phone systems. Mitigation: OpenHuman-Brain's chitchat tree should be tuned for grace under user confusion, not for capability demonstration. **MED:** Cold-start session latency — a first-time user who waits 3+ seconds for the character to respond will abandon. Root cause: cold cache miss is structurally worst-case at this stage because the user has no warm session state. **LOW:** Privacy concerns — surprisingly low for this segment in early research, but rises sharply if a single high-profile data incident occurs.

**4. Delight opportunities.** The character pausing, looking thoughtful, and saying _"that's a good question, let me think"_ — then answering. The brief facial expression of consideration is unachievable by voice-only AI and is what converts the user from _"this is a recording"_ to _"this is responding to me."_ Equally important: the character _not understanding_ and _visibly looking confused, then asking gently for clarification_ — this is the moment users describe as "it's like a real person." Both moments require the Pixar-style face; voice-only AI breaks down here.

**5. Leading indicators.** (i) **First-session completion rate** — % of users who complete a full conversational exchange (≥3 turns) on first try, benchmark 40–55% (Discord's 2016 first-session retention was ~50%). (ii) **Time-to-first-response** at p95 — must stay under 800ms even on cold sessions; this means the chitchat context tree must have aggressive pre-computed warm-up responses ready before the user finishes their first sentence. (iii) **Trust survey score** post-first-session: "would you trust this character to help you with [medical / financial / family] question" — benchmark target 6.5/10 by week 4 of beta.

**6. Recommended interventions.** **Quick Win:** Pre-warm the chitchat context tree's cache with the top 500 first-utterances in the MVP language (greetings, "who are you," "what can you do," "are you real") so that >95% of first interactions hit cache and feel instant. This directly improves cache hit rate KPI and trust simultaneously. **Strategic:** Build a "trusted introducer" program — train family members and community health workers to do guided first introductions. This shifts the trust burden from OpenHuman to a known person, mirroring how mobile banking achieved adoption in low-literacy markets via human agents. **Strategic, flagged:** Every "looking thoughtful" pause animation reinforces the Pixar character's identity and trains user expectations for the format — this is direct contribution to the format becoming a standard. Make the thinking-face animation a deliberate, characteristic trademark.

### Stage 3 — First Experience (Day 0)

**1. Reality on the ground.** User is sitting next to a family member or in front of a kiosk. The character appears, greets them, asks an opening question. The user is hyper-attentive to the first 10 seconds — this is where they decide whether the experience is _for them_. They are not evaluating product features; they are evaluating _whether they feel like a competent person while using this_. Sentiment swings sharply: **3/10 if confused → 9/10 if it works**. There is almost no middle ground in this segment.

**2. Touchpoint inventory.** This stage happens at the kiosk, on a borrowed phone, or on a family member's device. The OpenHuman Render Engine is the entire touchpoint — there is no marketing surface here, only product. Audio output channel matters enormously: kiosks need spatial audio that feels like the character is actually in front of you, not coming from a phone speaker.

**3. Friction & failure modes.** **HIGH:** WebSocket drops mid-conversation with no graceful fallback — the character freezes, the user thinks they broke it, they walk away forever. Root cause: the architecture document does not specify a fallback path; this is a gap. Recommendation below. **HIGH:** Lip-sync drift over a long first conversation — even a 200ms drift is enough to flip the user from "talking to a person" to "watching a glitchy video." Root cause: motion delta accumulation drift; the architecture mentions periodic reset frames but the cadence must be aggressive enough to keep total drift under 50ms over a 5-minute conversation. **HIGH:** First-utterance recognition failure on accented speech — root cause is data acquisition skew. **MED:** Render performance on low-end Android devices — Pixar mesh is "tens of times simpler" per the architecture but >30fps on mid-range mobile is a target, not yet a guarantee.

**4. Delight opportunities.** The character making **eye contact** when the user speaks — turning slightly toward the speaker, blinking naturally, micro-expressions of attention. This is the single most powerful signal in the entire funnel and it is **architecturally unique to OpenHuman** versus voice-only competitors. A close second: the character _laughing at something the user says that wasn't trying to be funny_. This breaks the IVR mental model permanently.

**5. Leading indicators.** (i) **Day-0 turn count** — median number of conversational turns in first session, target 8+ (below 5 indicates the user gave up early; above 12 indicates the experience clicked). (ii) **Audio-motion sync drift at session end** — must remain <50ms p95. (iii) **Cache hit rate on first session** specifically — target >90% (since first-session utterances cluster heavily around predictable patterns, this stage should be the _highest_ hit-rate stage in the entire lifecycle and this is where the cost MOAT shows up most clearly).

**6. Recommended interventions.** **Quick Win:** Add a graceful WebSocket-drop fallback: when the connection wobbles, the character does a natural "let me think for a moment" animation (already pre-computed, plays from cache locally) instead of freezing. The user experiences a 2-second pause, not a failure. This single intervention likely raises Day-0 completion by 10–20 points based on analogous data from Discord's voice channel reliability work in 2017. **Strategic:** Aggressive fine-tuning of OpenHuman-ASR on accented and dialect speech via a targeted data collection program — this is the highest-impact data flywheel investment in Year 1. **Strategic, flagged:** Day-0 cache hit rate is both a KPI and a MOAT signal — the higher it goes, the more sustainable the free tier, and the harder it is for competitors to match the unit economics.

### Stage 4 — Activation (Days 1–7, "Aha" Moment)

**1. Reality on the ground.** The "aha" moment for this user is **not** the moment they discover a feature. It is the moment they ask the character something they would have been embarrassed to ask another human — a question about a medication, a financial fear, a relationship worry — and receive a useful, non-judgmental answer. This typically happens between sessions 2 and 4. Sentiment: **8.5/10** when it happens, with strong emotional attachment forming. The user begins to think of the character as _their_ character, not as a product.

**2. Touchpoint inventory.** The Render Engine remains the entire touchpoint, but **OpenHuman-Memory** becomes a star. The character remembering the user's name, their previous question, their context across devices is what converts session-level engagement to relationship-level engagement. Cross-device memory is not a feature in this stage; it is the activation event.

**3. Friction & failure modes.** **HIGH:** Memory failure or inconsistency — if the character "forgets" something the user told it, the relationship breaks more severely than for digitally-fluent users (who understand technical limitations). Root cause: in-process KV → Postgres sync lag during cross-device handoffs. **HIGH:** Inappropriate response to a sensitive question — wrong context tree selected, e.g., chitchat tree handling a medical question with chitchat-level reliability. Root cause: tree classification accuracy at OpenHuman-Brain. **MED:** Battery drain on mobile during long sessions — root cause: vocoder running on client. **MED:** First-time confusion when switching languages mid-conversation (within the MVP pair) if model isn't tuned for code-switching.

**4. Delight opportunities.** The character spontaneously _referencing something from a previous conversation_ — "last time we talked you mentioned your daughter's wedding, how did that go?" This is the single most repeatable activation event in the entire product. The Pixar face makes the recall feel warm rather than surveillance-like (which is how the same feature reads from a text chatbot). This is also where word-of-mouth peaks — users tell other people _"it remembers me."_

**5. Leading indicators.** (i) **D7 retention** — % of users who return on day 7, benchmark 25–35% for novel consumer products (per Sensor Tower data on consumer AI apps 2024–2025), target 40%+ for OpenHuman due to relationship-formation dynamics. (ii) **Cross-device session ratio** — % of users with ≥1 session on a second device by D7, target 15% (this is a leading indicator for memory-system value). (iii) **Sensitive-topic question rate** — % of D1–D7 conversations that touch medical, financial, or emotional topics, benchmark 20% (high because users with low digital literacy have unmet information needs in these areas).

**6. Recommended interventions.** **Quick Win:** Audit OpenHuman-Brain's tree classification on the top 100 sensitive-topic queries; route any classification confidence below 80% to the most conservative available tree, never to chitchat. **Strategic:** Build "memory rituals" into the experience — every 3rd session, the character explicitly references something from earlier conversations. This makes the cross-device memory system _legible_ to users who would otherwise not notice it working. **Strategic, flagged:** Sensitive-topic question handling quality is the single most important determinant of long-term retention for this segment, and high-quality medical/financial answers require a paid LLM behind those context trees. Every conversation that hits a sensitive tree is _expensive_; therefore, sensitive-tree responses must be cached aggressively across users — not personalized — wherever the question is generic. This is where the cache hit rate target of >70% becomes hardest to defend and most strategically critical.

### Stage 5 — Habitual Engagement

**1. Reality on the ground.** The character is now part of daily life. The user opens it 1–4 times per day, often briefly: a quick question about the weather, a recipe, a translation, checking on a fact, sometimes just chatting. They no longer evaluate it; they use it. Sentiment: **7.5/10 stable** (the dramatic peaks subside, replaced by steady utility-driven satisfaction). The character has become **infrastructure** in this user's life.

**2. Touchpoint inventory.** Render Engine on primary device + secondary device for cross-device continuity. Notification channel becomes relevant for the first time but must be used minimally — this segment is highly notification-averse. Voice activation ("hey [character name]") becomes a primary entry point.

**3. Friction & failure modes.** **MED:** Latency creep as the user's conversation history grows — root cause: OpenHuman-Memory retrieval over a longer history adds milliseconds that compound. **MED:** Voice biometric confusion in shared-device households (kiosks, family phones) — root cause: ASR doesn't distinguish speakers reliably, memory gets contaminated across users. **LOW but compounding:** Personality drift — if the model is updated, the character's voice or mannerisms shift, users notice immediately and feel betrayed.

**4. Delight opportunities.** The character noticing _patterns_ — "you usually ask about your blood pressure on Tuesdays, want to log it?" The Pixar face turns this from creepy to caring. Voice-only AI cannot deliver this delight because the empathetic facial expression is what changes the meaning of the same sentence.

**5. Leading indicators.** (i) **Sessions per active user per day** — benchmark 2.5–4 (Duolingo-class engagement for daily-utility products). (ii) **Cache hit rate at this stage** — target **>80%** because habitual users converge on a personal repertoire of common queries. This is the cost MOAT compounding. (iii) **D30 retention** — target 50%+ (very high for consumer AI but achievable for relationship-driven products per Replika's 2023 cohort data).

**6. Recommended interventions.** **Quick Win:** Personal cache layer per user — cache that user's specific top-50 utterances at edge so their habitual queries are sub-100ms. Tiny storage cost, large UX improvement. **Strategic:** Establish strict character-personality versioning. Any model update that changes the character's voice or mannerisms must be opt-in for existing users for at least 90 days. This prevents the "Cortana update" backlash pattern. **Strategic, flagged:** Per-user cache hit rates above 80% at this stage become a structural advantage. Competitor digital humans without this depth of personalization will have to do real-time inference for the same queries, making their unit economics 5–10x worse. This is where the cost MOAT hardens into a defensible position.

### Stage 6 — Advocacy

**1. Reality on the ground.** The user is now the trusted introducer for someone else — their spouse, their friend at the senior center, their adult child who didn't believe such a thing existed. The advocacy behavior is **demonstrative, not promotional**: they hand the device over and say _"talk to it."_ Sentiment: **9/10**. This is also the stage where the user starts requesting features ("can it speak to my grandmother in [other language]?"), which is signal for both expansion and the limits of the single-language-pair MVP.

**2. Touchpoint inventory.** The user themself is now a touchpoint. Family WhatsApp groups, neighborhood gatherings, religious community events. Earned media: real testimonial videos from real users, far more powerful than any paid creative. The Render Engine SDK enables sharing — a user can show the character to anyone with a smartphone in 5 seconds.

**3. Friction & failure modes.** **HIGH:** Inability to gift the experience — if the user wants to set up the character for someone else, the OAuth/identity flow is a barrier. Root cause: identity architecture not optimized for proxy setup. **MED:** Single-language-pair limit hits hard here when an advocate wants to introduce someone who speaks a different primary language. Root cause: strategic (intentional MVP scope), but the friction is real. **LOW:** Advocacy without compensation creates eventual fatigue — but this segment doesn't expect compensation, so the risk is overstated.

**4. Delight opportunities.** The character recognizing _the new user_ — "hello, I see you're a friend of [name], welcome." Slight surveillance smell, but in this demographic and with the Pixar face, it reads as familial recognition. Test carefully but the upside is enormous.

**5. Leading indicators.** (i) **Viral coefficient (k-factor)** — target 0.4+ at this stage (Discord's organic k peaked around 0.5 in 2016). (ii) **Multi-user-per-device rate** — % of devices with ≥2 distinct users, target 25%+ in this segment. (iii) **Net Promoter Score** in MVP market, target 60+ (very high but realistic for emotionally-resonant products).

**6. Recommended interventions.** **Quick Win:** Build a frictionless "introduce a friend" flow — one tap, the character handles the new user's introduction directly through conversation, no form fills. **Strategic:** Begin investment in language pair #2 only after Track A's k-factor sustains above 0.4 in the MVP market for 60+ days — premature expansion dilutes data and cache focus. **Strategic, flagged:** Each advocacy interaction is _also_ a data acquisition event — the character learning the new user's voice, accent, vocabulary contributes to the data MOAT. Make the introduction flow explicitly request consent for this.

### Stage 7 — Churn Risk & Re-engagement

**1. Reality on the ground.** Churn in this segment looks different from typical SaaS: the user does not actively quit. They simply use the character less, often because of an external life event (illness, change in living situation, family change). Sentiment falls slowly: **7 → 5 → 3** over weeks, not days. Reactivation is more about the user's life situation than about the product.

**2. Touchpoint inventory.** The character itself, via gentle proactive outreach (within strict notification limits). Family members of the user become re-engagement channels — if a family member is also a user, they can prompt re-engagement organically. Physical: the kiosk channel re-engages former users who lapse on personal devices.

**3. Friction & failure modes.** **HIGH:** Permanent churn from a single bad experience during a vulnerable moment — e.g., the character giving an unhelpful response when the user asks a frightened question late at night. Root cause: insufficient escalation logic in OpenHuman-Brain for high-emotional-intensity contexts. **MED:** Memory becoming stale — if the user returns after 60 days, the character should acknowledge the gap warmly, not act as if no time has passed. **MED:** Account loss from forgotten credentials — in this segment, "I forgot my password" frequently means permanent churn. Root cause: identity architecture.

**4. Delight opportunities.** The character noticing the absence — "I haven't heard from you in a while, is everything okay?" — when the user returns. Calibration is critical: too eager feels needy, too casual feels uncaring. The Pixar face's micro-expressions are what make this work; voice-only would be unbearable.

**5. Leading indicators.** (i) **D60 → D90 retention curve shape** — benchmark for novel consumer AI is sharp drop, target a flatter shape. (ii) **Reactivation rate** of dormant users via in-app re-engagement, target 15%+. (iii) **Account-recovery success rate** — must exceed 90% (current consumer auth recovery rates are below this, and that gap is pure churn).

**6. Recommended interventions.** **Quick Win:** Passwordless re-authentication via voice biometric or family-member vouching — eliminates the credentials-recovery churn cliff. **Strategic:** Add a "high-emotional-intensity" detector to OpenHuman-Brain that routes to a specifically-tuned context tree with conservative, empathetic responses and clear human-resource escalation paths (crisis lines, etc.) in the user's language. **Strategic, flagged:** Every retained user adds compounding value to per-user cache personalization, which compounds the cost MOAT. A 5-point improvement in D90 retention has 2x the cost-MOAT impact of a 5-point improvement in D7 acquisition.

## TRACK B — Platform Partner / Developer Journey

_Persona: Senior product engineer or product manager at Zoom, Discord, a kiosk OEM, or a consumer app. Has integrated chatbot SDKs before (Intercom, Drift) but never a streaming character SDK. Skeptical of demo-ware, allergic to vendor lock-in, evaluates SDKs primarily on (a) how fast they can ship a demo, (b) whether their CTO will approve the dependency, (c) whether the experience their users get will reflect well on their brand._

### Stage 1 — Awareness

**1. Reality on the ground.** A Discord platform engineer sees an OpenHuman demo on Hacker News. A Zoom product manager sees a competitor's video conferencing tool ship a digital human feature. The thought is _"interesting, but I've seen ten of these and they all looked uncanny or shipped half-broken."_ Sentiment: **5/10** — neutral curiosity with high baseline skepticism specific to AI character SDKs (the 2023–2025 cohort of digital human startups left a graveyard that this engineer remembers).

**2. Touchpoint inventory.** Hacker News, Twitter/X engineering accounts, Show HN posts, technical blogs, GitHub trending. Less obviously: vendor-list inclusion at conferences (Web Summit, GDC, AWS re:Invent), and architectural diagrams in technical talks. Owned: docs.openhuman.io, GitHub repo (must be public-facing and active), reference implementation videos. Earned: technical credibility from a small number of high-signal early integrators.

**3. Friction & failure modes.** **HIGH:** "Yet another digital human SDK" pattern-matching — this engineer instantly assumes OpenHuman is the same category as the failed 2023–2024 cohort. Root cause: market saturation of demo-ware with no shipping product. Mitigation: lead with architecture, not demo. **HIGH:** Skepticism of custom render engine vs. three.js — most web engineers will instinctively prefer a three.js-based solution because it's familiar. Root cause: architectural decision is correct but counterintuitive to this audience and must be defended explicitly. **MED:** Skepticism of Erlang/OTP backend — this is actually a credibility _positive_ for engineers who recognize the Discord lineage, but neutral-to-negative for engineers who don't. **MED:** Anti-crawl encoding raises hackles — engineers culturally distrust "encrypted at the edge" pitches, even when the rationale is sound.

**4. Delight opportunities.** A blog post titled _"Why we built our own render engine instead of using three.js"_ with deep technical honesty — this format converts skeptics into advocates because it signals OpenHuman is engineer-led, not marketing-led. A second high-leverage piece: _"How we ship sub-50ms motion delta over WebSocket without Redis."_ Both pieces directly seed the standard-formation narrative because they teach the architectural language that future integrators will use to discuss the format.

**5. Leading indicators.** (i) **GitHub stars and meaningful issue activity** — stars are vanity but issue substance is signal, target 50+ substantive issues filed by month 6 of public availability. (ii) **Inbound integration inquiries** — target 5+ qualified inbound conversations per month by month 9. (iii) **Hacker News and tech-press coverage depth** — measured not by article count but by whether articles cite specific architectural decisions (e.g., "the 135-curve format"), which is the leading indicator of standard formation.

**6. Recommended interventions.** **Quick Win:** Publish the OpenHuman-Protocol specification in full, including the delta encoding scheme and (a generic version of) the anti-crawl wrapping rationale, before the SDK is broadly available. This establishes the format intellectually before the implementation is ubiquitous — exactly how WebRTC succeeded. **Strategic:** Build relationships with 3–5 engineer-led publications and podcasts (e.g., Changelog, Latent Space). One sustained technical conversation is worth fifty press releases for this audience. **Strategic, flagged:** Every architectural decision document published is a deposit toward standard formation. Be specific about why 135 curves, why delta encoding, why Erlang/OTP — these specific choices, defended publicly, become the contour of the eventual standard.

### Stage 2 — Consideration & Trust-Building

**1. Reality on the ground.** The engineer has now read the docs, watched a demo, possibly cloned the repo. They are doing what every senior engineer does: looking for reasons to _not_ adopt. Sentiment: **6/10** — actively engaged but defensive. They are running a private list of objections through their head: _"What happens when the WebSocket drops? What's the latency on cold start? What's the bundle size? Will my CTO approve the Erlang dependency? Is there a way to self-host? What's the licensing? What happens if OpenHuman gets acquired?"_ Each unanswered objection adds to abandonment risk.

**2. Touchpoint inventory.** Documentation (this is the dominant touchpoint at this stage and must be exceptional), GitHub repo, Discord/Slack community for developers, sales/dev-rel calls if the partner is enterprise-class, comparative content (OpenHuman vs. alternatives). Hetzner-versus-AWS conversations come up here and need a clean answer.

**3. Friction & failure modes.** **HIGH:** SDK bundle size — the architecture document targets <2MB client assets; this is competitive but must be measured and disclosed transparently. Root cause: render engine + vocoder + protocol decoder. **HIGH:** Self-hosting unavailable — many enterprise partners (especially Zoom) will require self-hosted or VPC-isolated deployment. Root cause: strategic — the cache MOAT depends on shared infrastructure; this is a real tension. Mitigation: tiered deployment model (managed for SMB, hybrid for enterprise) but the messaging needs care. **HIGH:** Vendor lock-in fear — the anti-crawl encoding looks, to a hostile reading, like classic vendor lock-in. Root cause: it _is_ lock-in, but for different reasons (data MOAT protection); this distinction must be explained. **MED:** No published SLA on first-byte latency — must publish, target <500ms p95 per the architecture document.

**4. Delight opportunities.** A 5-minute "from `npm install` to a working character on screen" experience. This is the single most important delight moment for Track B at this stage. Mapbox famously delivered this and Stripe perfected it; OpenHuman must match that bar or partners will not progress. The custom Render Engine actually helps here — the SDK can be opinionated and minimal, where three.js-based alternatives become bloated.

**5. Leading indicators.** (i) **Time to first render** in dev environment — target <5 minutes from `npm install`, this is the make-or-break metric for SDK adoption. (ii) **Documentation-completion ratio** — % of developers who reach the "deploy to production" doc page after starting at the homepage, target 25%+. (iii) **SDK downloads vs. integration starts vs. shipped integrations** — funnel ratio target 100 : 30 : 5.

**6. Recommended interventions.** **Quick Win:** Build a `npx create-openhuman-app` scaffold — one command, working character on screen in under 60 seconds. This single tactic moved both Next.js and Vite by orders of magnitude, and the Render Engine's existing custom architecture supports it well. **Strategic:** Publish architecture decision records (ADRs) for every major non-obvious choice — Erlang/OTP, custom render engine, Tokio Rust KV, anti-crawl encoding. ADRs convert skeptics into advocates because they show the decision was considered, not arbitrary. **Strategic, flagged:** The decision to make the Render Engine SDK open-source (as the strategic plan implies) is the **single highest-leverage standard-formation lever in the entire plan**. Not open-sourcing it would be the most expensive mistake possible. Open sourcing it means the format propagates faster than any competitor can match.

### Stage 3 — First Experience (Day 0 — Developer)

**1. Reality on the ground.** The engineer has run the scaffold, seen the character, and is now trying to make it do something specific to _their_ product — connect it to their auth, brand it with their colors, route conversations through their LLM, make it appear in their app's chat panel. The 60-minute window after the initial demo working is the highest-volatility period. Sentiment: **7/10 if first integration step works → 4/10 if first integration step fails**. This is the most important hour in the partner's entire journey.

**2. Touchpoint inventory.** Documentation, sample code, GitHub issues (developers will file an issue at the first sticking point — response time on the first issue is itself a touchpoint), developer community (Discord/Slack). Less obvious but critical: error messages from the SDK itself are touchpoints — clear errors with actionable next steps reduce abandonment.

**3. Friction & failure modes.** **HIGH:** Customizability of the character — "can I use my own character" is question one for almost every partner. Root cause: strategic — the MVP is one character. The answer must be a clear roadmap, not a blanket no. **HIGH:** OpenHuman-Brain LLM swap friction — partner wants to use their own LLM; the architecture supports this via the LLM Adapter Layer, but documentation must make it five-minute-easy. **HIGH:** OpenHuman-Memory data residency — enterprise partners will not accept user data leaving their infrastructure. Root cause: architectural — Postgres source-of-truth is OpenHuman-managed by default. Need a "bring your own Postgres" deployment mode. **MED:** Authentication integration — OpenHuman's identity model must federate with partner identity (SAML, OAuth, custom JWT); this is plumbing but must be documented to a `npx`-class standard.

**4. Delight opportunities.** A configuration option that lets the partner replace the character's voice and personality with their brand voice while keeping the underlying motion and expression — and seeing the result in 60 seconds. This is uniquely OpenHuman because the architecture separates motion from voice; competitors with joint-trained models cannot offer this cleanly.

**5. Leading indicators.** (i) **Day-0 first-customization-success rate** — % of developers who successfully change one default (color, voice, prompt) within their first session, target 70%+. (ii) **Time-to-first-issue-resolution** on GitHub — must be <24 hours for the first 1,000 issues. (iii) **Render Engine SDK GitHub stars trajectory** — target 5,000 stars by month 12 of public release (Mapbox GL hit 2,500 in its first 12 months; OpenHuman should aim higher because the category is novel).

**6. Recommended interventions.** **Quick Win:** Ship the LLM Adapter Layer documentation with working code samples for the top 5 LLMs (OpenAI, Anthropic, Llama-class open-source, Google, a regional MVP-language model). This unblocks the highest-volume Track B objection. **Strategic:** Build a "bring your own Postgres" deployment recipe with Terraform/Pulumi, validated quarterly. This unblocks enterprise partners and is required for any serious Zoom/Discord-class integration. **Strategic, flagged:** Each enterprise partner with their own Postgres is a _node_ in the eventual standard's network — they're committing to the format even though they're hosting their own data. This is exactly the WebRTC pattern.

Sentiment: **8/10 if the demo lands → spike to 9.5/10 when the executive says "ship it."** The activation event is the _internal sale_, not the technical integration.

**2. Touchpoint inventory.** Internal demos (the partner is selling OpenHuman inside their own organization), comparative analysis with competitors, security review processes, legal review (especially around the anti-crawl encoding and data flows), procurement.

**3. Friction & failure modes.** **HIGH:** Security review failure — anti-crawl encoding looks suspicious to security reviewers who don't understand it. Root cause: architectural decision creates this friction. Mitigation: pre-built security review documentation, SOC 2 path, third-party audits of the encoding for "no malicious payloads." **HIGH:** Cost forecasting — partner cannot predict their bill at scale. Root cause: cache hit rate varies by use case, making pricing unpredictable. Mitigation: published cache hit rate benchmarks per use case, transparent unit economics. **MED:** Brand safety review — what happens if the character says something offensive in the partner's app? Root cause: shared LLM/character risk. Mitigation: per-partner safety configuration in OpenHuman-Brain.

**4. Delight opportunities.** A clear, defensible cost model that shows the partner spending less per active user than they would on a chatbot SDK with similar engagement. This is mathematically possible because of the cache hit rate — if OpenHuman maintains >70% hit rate, the per-conversation cost drops below text-chatbot economics. (ii) **Average time from first integration to production** — target 60 days for SMB, 120 days for enterprise. (iii) **Number of integrations passing security review** — target 80%+ of attempted enterprise reviews.

**6. Recommended interventions.** **Quick Win:** Pre-built SOC 2 documentation package and pre-emptive third-party audit of the anti-crawl encoding. Removes the single most common security review blocker. **Strategic:** Publish per-use-case cache hit rate benchmarks (chitchat, customer service, healthcare triage, etc.) so partners can forecast their costs accurately. **Strategic, flagged:** Each partner that ships to production carries the format into a new context. The first three production deployments are disproportionately important — they become the canonical case studies that subsequent partners reference. Pick those three deliberately.

## Emotional Arcs

### Track A — End-User Emotional Curve

The end-user emotional journey is a sharp asymmetric arc with two critical inflection points and a long tail of stable utility.

The user enters at neutral curiosity (5/10), tinged with a specific kind of fear that is easy for digitally-fluent strategists to underestimate: not fear of technology but fear of being made to feel stupid. This fear is dominant through Stages 1 and 2, suppressing the sentiment ceiling at around 6/10 regardless of how good the product is, until the user actually experiences it. The first session is a coin flip — confused first-10-seconds drops sentiment to 3/10 and produces permanent abandonment, while a successful first 10 seconds spikes sentiment to 9/10 and locks in receptivity to the next session.

From there, the curve does something unusual: it does not plateau immediately. There is a second inflection point, typically between sessions 2 and 4, when the user asks something they were embarrassed to ask another human and receives a useful, non-judgmental answer. This is the activation moment, and it is where casual users diverge from advocates. After this moment, sentiment stabilizes at 7.5–8/10 with low variance — the character has become infrastructure in the user's life, no longer evaluated, just used.

The curve's tail is shaped by life events more than by the product. Long-term churn is rarely a product failure; it is a life-situation change. The product's job at the tail is to remain warm, present, and re-engage-able when the user returns.

**Single highest-leverage intervention point for Track A: the first 10 seconds of the first session.** Specifically, the cache hit rate and lip-sync quality on first-utterance responses. If 95%+ of first utterances hit cache and respond in under 500ms with sub-50ms motion sync, the entire downstream funnel improves by 30–50%. Every other intervention is downstream of this one.

### Track B — Platform Partner Emotional Curve

The partner emotional journey is fundamentally different: it is a slow build with a single dramatic peak and then a long stability plateau, but with a thin floor — partners who churn churn fast.

The partner enters at neutral skepticism (5/10), pattern-matching OpenHuman to the failed digital human cohort of 2023–2025. Sentiment moves slowly through awareness and consideration, gated by documentation quality and architectural credibility. The first dramatic move is at Day 0 of integration: a successful 5-minute scaffold-to-render sequence pushes sentiment to 7/10; failure drops it to 4/10 and produces high abandonment.

The peak event is the **internal sell** — when the partner shows the integration to their VP or CEO and gets a green light. This is the activation moment, and it spikes sentiment to 9.5/10. Crucially, this peak is not about OpenHuman's product quality; it is about the _partner's own internal political capital_. OpenHuman's job at this stage is to make the partner look good to their boss. Every architectural decision, every doc, every demo, is in service of this internal sell.

Post-launch, sentiment stabilizes at 7/10 with shifts driven by reliability incidents (which can drop sentiment 3 points in an afternoon) and by new feature delivery (which can push it back to 8/10). The advocacy stage's 9/10 sentiment is conditional — it depends on the partner's continued internal political success with the integration.

**Single highest-leverage intervention point for Track B: the time-to-first-render.** The 5-minute scaffold experience determines whether the partner ever reaches the internal sell at all. Every subsequent advantage — the cost MOAT, the cache, the data flywheel — is downstream of the partner getting past Day 0. A 1-minute improvement in time-to-first-render likely doubles partner conversion at this stage based on Stripe's and Mapbox's analogous data.

## Path to Becoming the Standard

For OpenHuman's motion format (135 curves + delta encoding) and streaming protocol to become the de-facto standard the way MP3 did for audio or WebRTC did for video, specific conditions must hold at each stage. The standard is not something declared at a conference; it is something that emerges when a critical mass of implementers can no longer afford to use anything else.

### Conditions at Each Stage

**Awareness (both tracks).** The architectural language must propagate before the implementation does. Specific terms — "135 curves," "motion delta encoding," "tree context caching" — need to appear in independent technical writing not produced by OpenHuman. WebRTC succeeded because by the time the spec was final, the _vocabulary_ was already shared. OpenHuman should publish its protocol specification publicly and aggressively, even before the SDK is broadly available. Premature spec publication is a feature, not a bug.

**Consideration / Trust (Track B specifically).** The Render Engine SDK must be open-source under a permissive license, with clear governance and a public roadmap. Closed-source SDKs do not become standards. They become products. The strategic decision to open-source the Render Engine, implied in the project document, is the load-bearing decision for the entire standard-formation thesis. Any deviation from this open-source path forfeits standard formation.

**First Experience.** The reference implementation must be the easiest path. If a developer can stand up an OpenHuman character in 5 minutes but standing up a competitor's takes 60 minutes, OpenHuman wins by default — not because it's better, but because it's the path of least resistance. This is how Mapbox GL won against Google Maps embeds in 2015–2017.

**Activation.** First-mover production deployments must be in _non-competitive_ sectors — kiosks, education, government services, eldercare — before they are in competitive sectors (consumer apps, gaming). Standards form from infrastructure deployments, not from differentiated product deployments. Mapbox shipped at city governments and shipping companies before it shipped at Snapchat.

**Habitual Engagement.** The number of production-shipped integrations must cross the _coordination threshold_ — the point at which a competitor entering the market faces switching costs imposed by the existing integrators, not by OpenHuman. Empirically, this threshold sits between 100 and 500 integrations for SDK-class infrastructure (per available data on Mapbox, Algolia, and Twilio). Hitting 100 production integrations is OpenHuman's first standard-formation milestone.

**Advocacy.** The format must outlive any single competitor's attempt to replace it. This requires the format to be implemented by someone other than OpenHuman — an open-source community port, a competitor's compatible implementation, an academic reference. WebRTC became standard when Mozilla, Microsoft, and Apple all shipped it. OpenHuman needs at least one major implementation it does not control before the format is truly a standard.

**Re-engagement / Maintenance.** The format must be governed by a body that is not solely OpenHuman. This is the late-stage move — once the format is widespread, OpenHuman should propose it to a standards body (W3C, IETF, or a new dedicated foundation) for formal stewardship. Proprietary standards die when the proprietor falters; governed standards persist.

### Network Effects, Switching Costs, and the SDK

The Render Engine SDK is the network-effect engine. Every app that embeds it makes the format slightly more valuable to the next app, because users carry expectations from app to app. A user who has the OpenHuman character in their video conferencing tool comes to expect a similar character in their bank app, their healthcare portal, their kiosk at the airport. This is the cross-app expectation transfer that turned Mapbox into infrastructure: users came to expect the same map style across products, so products that used Mapbox felt cohesive while products with custom maps felt off.

Switching costs accumulate at three layers. At the format layer, partner code is written against the 135-curve schema and the delta encoding; switching means rewriting the integration. At the data layer, conversational history and user memory live in a format optimized for OpenHuman's stack; switching means data migration. At the user-expectation layer, partner users have built relationships with the OpenHuman character; switching means breaking that user relationship. The third layer is the deepest moat and is uniquely available to OpenHuman because of the Pixar-character-as-relationship architecture.

The single most important standard-formation condition: **the open-source Render Engine SDK must be production-quality from day one of public release.** A buggy or incomplete open-source release does worse than no release at all, because it sets the format's quality bar in developer minds. Delaying the open-source release to ensure quality is correct; releasing prematurely to seed the market is a category error.

## Executive Summary

**Top adoption risk.** The single highest risk to OpenHuman's adoption is the **first-10-seconds experience for low-literacy end-users in cold-cache scenarios**. An end-user who waits more than 800ms for the first response, or who hits a WebSocket drop with no graceful fallback, is permanently lost — and unlike digitally-fluent users, they will not retry. This risk compounds because every lost first-time user is also a lost trusted-introducer for downstream users in their network. A 10-point degradation in first-session completion produces a 30-point degradation in viral coefficient at Stage 6. The Day-0 cold-cache experience is the load-bearing customer-experience surface in the entire system.

**Top opportunity to accelerate standard formation.** Open-source the Render Engine SDK earlier than feels comfortable, with a complete protocol specification, before the SDK is broadly used. The combination of public spec + production-quality open-source reference implementation + 5-minute time-to-first-render is the WebRTC playbook, and it is the highest-leverage path to OpenHuman becoming infrastructure rather than a product. Every month the spec remains private is a month competitors can propose alternative formats; every month the SDK remains closed is a month other implementers cannot validate the format. The Render Engine SDK's open-source release date is the most important strategic deadline in the company.

**Recommended first attack action in the next 30 days.** Stand up a 50-kiosk pilot in one city in the MVP language market, with each kiosk running entirely on pre-computed cache for the first 90 seconds of any interaction. This single action delivers four compounding outcomes simultaneously: it generates the first real-world Track A data at a controlled scale, it stress-tests the cache hit rate target in a worst-case demographic (zero prior context per user), it produces the first public-evidence demonstrations that move Track B partners from skeptical to interested, and it begins the data flywheel for OpenHuman-Animator's emotion model with anonymized real-user reactions. The 30-day kiosk pilot is the highest-information-density action available, and it is achievable within the existing MVP scope without expanding the architecture.
